A Study on Happy Places, Seattle

In this project, observe an emotion in connection to a space in Seattle. People have the ability to understand and share the feelings of another. Revisiting a place with a good memory often brings back a smile to oneself. It also gives a smile to others. Those stories make things in the place meaningful and make us attached to the place in a positive manner. Do you agree?

What is a Happy Place?

Throughout life, we have visited many places and done many things. Sometimes, those things that we do or the events that occur mean so much to us that just thinking about that place often puts a smile on our face. That same place, however, could also give a smile to others as they may not share the same experience but share the same place too. Those eventful stories make places meaningful, thus making us attached to the place in a positive manner. Here in this project, we will be studying which places in Seattle have been chosen as my peers' “Happy Place”, and what made them choose so. This project was inspired by “Imagining the Details: Happy Places and Creative Geovisualization” by Jin-Kyu Jung and Ted Hiebert.

Data Collection & Method

The data was compiled using the Padlet map service where students posted about their Happy Place and a description of why it is their Happy Place. The data was then extracted as csv for processing. We used google colab to process the data, where we used an atlas of neighborhoods in Seattle to spatially join to the dataset so that each row has the corresponding neighborhood. We then filtered out the common words to leave only the more meaningful words behind that actually describe the Happy Place. We then used that cleaned and summarized description to create the infographic.

The infographic I decided to use is the tree map chart because I wanted the infographic to display the words used by the authors and how they described their Happy Place. The amount of words used to describe the place also became a scale of the richness of the author's relationship with the location, and the amount of text entries within each neighborhood shows how many people chose a Happy Place within that neighborhood. Thus, by looking at the number of entries, we can learn which neighborhood has more intricate connections to my peers.

Findings and Conclusion

It was not surprising that most of the Happy Places were within the University District because we spend most of our time here within the school grounds, but each description for their Happy Place was very different. Different people enjoyed different sides of the University District, from studying in the Suzzallo library to playing football at the IMA field. From the infographic we can observe the neighborhoods that include our Happy Places and read each peer’s description of why they chose the place and what event may have happened that led them to having a deeper connection to that specific place compared to others. It was very interesting to read each student’s story and learn more about them during this assignment.

Thoughts & Reflection

Since the only group portion of this assignment was creating the story, my role for the rest of the project was to extract the data, process it and create the infographic. This assignment was not that complicated as we were provided with more than enough google colab notebooks that contained steps of how to process that data and how to create the various different types of infographics. Not only did I learn more about data procession and the different infographics, but in also learned more about my peers and their Happy Place stories. The only negative aspect was understanding the lines of codes in the different colab notebooks and bringing them together to finish this lab assignment. One goal to improve this experience is to hopefully fully understand the context of the codes provided so that the assignment can go smoothly if it were to be done as a group rather than individually.
